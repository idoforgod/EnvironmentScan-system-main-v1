## 6. Plausible Scenarios

**Scenario A: Cascading Geopolitical Crisis (25% probability)**
The Kharg Island strikes trigger Iranian retaliation (Hormuz closure or Gulf state infrastructure attacks), creating a global energy crisis. Oil prices exceed $150/barrel. Combined with WHO withdrawal and AGOA termination, the US faces unprecedented international isolation. The energy crisis accelerates clean energy investment but also triggers recession in energy-importing economies. AI governance remains fragmentary as nations prioritize security spending. Climate adaptation funding is diverted to military budgets, leaving developing nations doubly exposed (no health cooperation, no trade access, no climate support).

**Scenario B: Contained Disruption, Accelerated Transition (40% probability)**
Kharg Island damage is contained; Iran seeks diplomatic channels through intermediaries. Oil prices stabilize within 4-6 weeks at elevated but manageable levels. The crisis creates a "Sputnik moment" for energy transition: EU accelerates wind deployment, China executes 15th FYP ahead of schedule, and even the US accelerates domestic clean energy under energy security framing. AI governance remains in interregnum but no major incidents occur. AGOA-affected African nations accelerate AfCFTA implementation, partially compensating for lost US market access.

**Scenario C: Fragmented Normalization (25% probability)**
Multiple crises normalize without resolution. Kharg Island becomes a frozen conflict (damaged but partially restored). WHO operates at reduced capacity without the US. AGOA termination becomes permanent without replacement. AI governance vacuum persists as all jurisdictions avoid comprehensive regulation. Climate extremes intensify but elicit incremental rather than transformative responses. The "new normal" is characterized by higher baseline risk, weaker institutions, and greater reliance on bilateral rather than multilateral mechanisms.

**Scenario D: Crisis-Driven Cooperation Revival (10% probability)**
The severity of simultaneous crises (energy, health, climate, trade) creates political pressure for renewed multilateral cooperation. A new pandemic threat or a major AI safety incident provides the catalyst for US re-engagement with WHO and consideration of AI governance frameworks. G7 or G20 summit produces emergency energy, health, and technology cooperation agreements. This scenario requires a US domestic political shift that appears unlikely in the current environment but cannot be excluded.

### 8.4 FSSF Classification Methodology

The Future Signal Scanning Framework (FSSF) classifies signals into 8 types based on their maturity, impact potential, and predictability. This classification is applied to all WF4 signals alongside STEEPs categorization.

| FSSF Type | Definition | Priority Level |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Weak Signal** | Faint, early indicator of potential future change; low confidence but high strategic value if confirmed | CRITICAL |
| **Wild Card** | High-impact, low-probability event that disrupts established patterns; typically unpredictable | CRITICAL |
| **Discontinuity** | Structural break from established trajectory; fundamentally alters existing systems or relationships | CRITICAL |
| **Driver** | Force or factor that actively shapes the direction and pace of change in a system | HIGH |
| **Emerging Issue** | Developing situation with potential to escalate; not yet fully formed but showing momentum | HIGH |
| **Precursor Event** | Early manifestation or leading indicator of a larger pattern or trend; concrete evidence of emerging change | HIGH |
| **Trend** | Confirmed direction of change with quantitative evidence; established momentum | MEDIUM |
| **Megatrend** | Large-scale, sustained direction of change spanning decades; structural force | MEDIUM |

Classification is performed by analyzing signal content against FSSF criteria including: evidence strength, impact scope, predictability, time horizon, and systemic implications.
